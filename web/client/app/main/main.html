Monocle

Sight for the Blind

Purpose

In the United States, there are over 7 million blind, color-blind, or visually-impaired adults. With color-blindness or levels of visual impairment, simple tasks like picking a matching outfit, knowing if it's daytime, or even walking around can be difficult to manage.

With Monocle, we hope to simplify color-recognition and navigating the environment by providing color, orientation, and ambient light information

Features

Using an Intel Edison, rgb LEDs, photoresistors, laser diodes, tilt sensors, and a bluetooth speaker, the Monocle glasses allow for easy color differentiation by correlating the light produced by the rgb LED when reflected off objects the wearer is looking at. The resulting color observed is then spoken back to the wearer through the bluetooth speaker, allowing even totally blind persons to know the color of what they're observing.

A tilt sensor is used to determine the angle of the wearer's view, to adjust for looking directly at the sky or at indoor lighting. A photoresistor relays information about the ambient light in a room, so the wearer can be told if they enter or leave a building. FInally, a red laser diode makes it easy to point out things in the field of view that are too far away for Monocle to sense, so others can step in and identify objects.

Monocle provides audible, accurate color identification, freeing users from being fully dependent on others, or stuck guessing the colors of objects on their own. With work, Monocle can be improved to sense even more about the environment using advanced data models, eventually discerning even more colors, explaining more about objects and conditions, and proving a useful health tool for the visually-impaired.
